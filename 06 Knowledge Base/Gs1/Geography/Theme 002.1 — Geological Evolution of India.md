# Fundamental Question

> Why does India look the way it does today?

India's present physiography is the cumulative outcome of billions of years of geological evolution involving crustal formation, continental fragmentation, plate movement, volcanism, mountain building, denudation and deposition. Every physiographic division is a consequence of this evolutionary history.

---

# Core Principle

**Present physiography is a product of past geology.**

Modern India's mountains, plateaus, plains, deserts and coasts cannot be understood without understanding their geological evolution.

---

# Evolution Timeline

## Stage I — Formation of the Indian Shield

- Formation of ancient continental crust.
- Development of stable cratons.
- Predominantly igneous and metamorphic rocks.
- Represents the oldest surviving part of India.

### Modern Evidence

→ Peninsular Plateau

---

## Stage II — Gondwana Phase

- Indian Plate formed part of Gondwanaland.
- Long period of tectonic stability.
- Deposition of Gondwana sediments.

### Legacy

- Coal-bearing Gondwana basins
- Stable Peninsular India

---

## Stage III — Break-up of Gondwanaland

- Indian Plate separated.
- Began rapid northward movement.
- Indian Ocean progressively opened.

### Consequence

Independent Indian Plate began its journey towards Eurasia.

---

## Stage IV — Deccan Volcanism

- Massive fissure eruptions.
- Extensive basaltic lava flows.

### Legacy

- Deccan Traps
- Black Cotton Soil (later weathering)
- Volcanic Plateau

---

## Stage V — Himalayan Orogeny

- Collision between Indian Plate and Eurasian Plate.
- Compression of marine sediments.
- Formation of young fold mountains.

### Consequences

- Himalayas
- Tibetan Plateau uplift
- Foreland basin formation

---

## Stage VI — Formation of Northern Plains

Continuous Himalayan uplift

↓

Weathering & erosion

↓

Sediment transport

↓

Alluvial deposition

↓

Indo-Gangetic-Brahmaputra Plain

---

## Stage VII — Ongoing Geological Evolution

India is still evolving.

Examples:

- Himalayan uplift
- Earthquakes
- River erosion
- Sedimentation
- Coastal evolution
- Delta growth

Geological evolution is therefore an ongoing process rather than a completed event.
