
## Part A – Geological Evolution and Structural Framework

> "The Peninsular Plateau is India's geological core. Its present physiography is the cumulative outcome of three major tectonic events followed by hundreds of millions of years of denudation."

---

# 1. Theme Essence

Unlike the Himalayas, which are young constructional mountains, the Peninsular Plateau is an ancient continental shield composed predominantly of Precambrian crystalline rocks. It has remained tectonically stable for hundreds of millions of years and its present landscape is primarily controlled by ancient tectonic events and prolonged differential erosion.

# 3. The Three Geological Events that Shaped the Peninsular Plateau

## I. Western Margin Faulting

### Geological Event

During the breakup of Gondwanaland, the western continental margin underwent faulting and subsidence towards the Arabian Sea.

This produced a steep western escarpment.

### Landforms Created

- Western Ghats
- West Coast Escarpment
- General eastward tilt of Peninsular India

### Long-Term Consequences

Climate

- Orographic rainfall
- Rain-shadow region

Drainage

- Eastward flowing rivers
- Western Ghats become the watershed

Economy

- Plantation agriculture
- Hydroelectric potential

---

## II. Narmada–Tapti Rift Formation

### Geological Event

Ancient crustal faulting created two major rift valleys.

- Narmada
- Tapti

### Landforms Created

- Rift valleys
- Structural depressions

### Long-Term Consequences

- Structural division between Central Highlands and Deccan Plateau.
- Narmada and Tapi become west-flowing rivers.
- Rift-controlled drainage.

---

## III. Deccan Trap Volcanism

### Geological Event

Around 66 Ma the Indian Plate crossed the Réunion Hotspot.

Massive fissure eruptions covered western India with successive basaltic lava flows.

### Landforms Created

- Deccan Traps
- Basaltic Plateau
- Step-like topography

### Long-Term Consequences

Soils

- Black Cotton Soil

Agriculture

- Cotton
- Sugarcane
- Soybean

Geomorphology

- Waterfalls
- Resistant basaltic terrain

---

# 4. Why Didn't the Peninsular Plateau Fold?

## Himalayas

- Collision zone
- Compressible Tethys sediments
- Fold Mountains
- Active tectonics

## Peninsular Plateau

- Stable shield
- Hard crystalline rocks
- Away from principal collision
- Resistant to compression

Therefore,

The Himalayas were constructed by compression, whereas the Peninsular Plateau remained structurally stable.

---

# 5. Why Isn't the Plateau Flat?

Although tectonically stable,

millions of years of

- weathering,
- river erosion,
- differential erosion,
- fault-controlled denudation,

produced

- escarpments,
- waterfalls,
- residual hills,
- valleys,
- plateaus,
- rift valleys.

Thus,

the Peninsular Plateau is best described as a **denudational landscape**.

- Hydropower

---

# 2. Drainage System

## Why do most Peninsular rivers flow east?

### Geological Cause

The **western margin faulting** created the Western Ghats and imparted a **general eastward slope** to the Peninsular Plateau.

Consequently,

- Godavari
- Krishna
- Kaveri
- Mahanadi

flow eastwards towards the Bay of Bengal.

### Consequences

- Long river courses
- Large catchment areas
- Extensive delta formation
- Higher irrigation potential

---

## Why do Narmada and Tapi flow west?

Unlike other Peninsular rivers, these are **structurally controlled rivers**.

They occupy the **Narmada–Tapti Rift Valley**, whose structural gradient is westward.

Thus,

they ignore the general eastward slope of the plateau.

### Systems Chain

Ancient Faulting

↓

Narmada–Tapti Rift

↓

Structural Depression

↓

Westward Flow

↓

Arabian Sea

# 6. Central Highlands

the Central Highlands consist of **fragmented structural uplands** rather than one continuous plateau.

### Geological Signature

Northern extension of Deccan Trap basalt.

### Characteristics

- Black Soil
- High moisture retention

---

## Bundelkhand Plateau

### Geological Signature

Ancient granitic shield.

### Characteristics

- Thin rocky soils
- Low groundwater recharge
- Seasonal rivers
- Frequent droughts

---
## Baghelkhand Plateau

### Geological Signature

The **Baghelkhand Plateau** represents a **transitional dissected plateau** between the Bundelkhand Plateau and the Chota Nagpur Plateau. It is characterized by a combination of **Vindhyan sedimentary formations** in the north and **ancient crystalline rocks** in the south.

Unlike Chota Nagpur, it did not undergo extensive magmatic intrusion and hydrothermal mineralization, resulting in comparatively limited economically significant mineral deposits.

---

### Physiographic Characteristics

- Transitional plateau between Bundelkhand and Chota Nagpur.
- Dominated by ancient denudational topography.
- Strongly dissected by the **Son River** and its tributaries.
- Moderate elevation with rugged relief.
- Composed of Vindhyan sedimentary rocks along with older crystalline formations.

---

### Functional Significance

The Son River and its tributaries have extensively eroded the plateau, producing a highly dissected landscape. Consequently, Baghelkhand acts as an important transitional physiographic region connecting the Central Highlands with the eastern Peninsular Plateau.

---

## Part C – Western Ghats, Eastern Ghats & Regional Synthesis

> *The Western and Eastern Ghats form the physiographic margins of the Peninsular Plateau. Although both originated from the same ancient Indian Shield, differences in tectonic evolution, erosion and drainage have produced two contrasting mountain systems that profoundly influence India's climate, biodiversity and economy.*

---

# 1. Western Ghats – The Physiographic Controller of Peninsular India

The Western Ghats are the single most influential physiographic feature of Peninsular India.

They regulate:

- Climate
- Drainage
- Biodiversity
- Agriculture
- Hydropower
- Human Settlements

Hence,

> **The Western Ghats are to Peninsular India what the Himalayas are to Northern India.**

---

# Geological Origin

During the breakup of Gondwanaland,

the western continental margin underwent faulting.

The western block subsided towards the Arabian Sea,

while the eastern block remained elevated,

forming a steep fault escarpment called the **Western Ghats**.

---

# Why are the Western Ghats Continuous?

- Formed as a single fault escarpment.
- Short west-flowing rivers have little opportunity to dissect them.
- Resistant crystalline rocks preserve the escarpment.

---

# Functional Significance

## Climate

Western Ghats intercept the Arabian Sea branch of the Southwest Monsoon.

### Consequences

- Heavy orographic rainfall.
- Wet western coastal belt.
- Rain-shadow over Deccan Interior.

---

## Drainage

Western Ghats form the principal watershed of Peninsular India.

Most major Peninsular rivers originate here.

Consequences

- Perennial river systems.
- Irrigation.
- Drinking water.
- Reservoir development.

---

## Biodiversity

Reasons

- Heavy rainfall.
- Altitudinal variation.
- Evergreen forests.
- Shola ecosystem.
- High endemism.

Hence,

Western Ghats constitute one of the world's biodiversity hotspots.

---

## Agriculture

Supports plantation agriculture.

Examples

- Tea
- Coffee
- Rubber
- Pepper
- Cardamom

---

## Hydropower

↓

High hydraulic head

↓

Waterfalls

↓

Hydroelectric projects

# 2. Eastern Ghats

Unlike the Western Ghats,

the Eastern Ghats are

- lower,
- discontinuous,
- highly dissected.

---

# Why are they discontinuous?

### Geological Cause

They are ancient denudational highlands.

### Geomorphic Cause

Major east-flowing rivers

- Mahanadi
- Godavari
- Krishna
- Kaveri

have cut across them for millions of years.

Result

↓

Deep river valleys

↓

Fragmented hill ranges

---

# Systems Chain

Ancient Highlands

↓

River Incision

↓

Differential Erosion

↓

Discontinuous Eastern Ghats

---

# Superimposed Drainage

The east-flowing rivers largely maintained their established courses while erosion exposed the underlying resistant rocks.

Hence,

they cut across the Eastern Ghats instead of flowing around them.

multiple hill systems converge.

- Nilgiri Hills

# Why are the highest peaks located here?

### Primary Cause

Convergence of

- Western Ghats

creates a broad elevated mountain mass.

### Secondary Cause

Ancient crystalline rocks resisted erosion,

preserving high elevations.
