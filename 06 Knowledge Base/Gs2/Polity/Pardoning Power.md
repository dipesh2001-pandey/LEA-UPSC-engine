# Purpose

The Pardoning Power enables the President to grant constitutional clemency after judicial proceedings have concluded.

It is **not an appellate judicial power**, but a constitutional mechanism to temper justice with mercy, humanity and public interest.

---

# Constitutional Philosophy

The Judiciary answers:

> **"What does the law require?"**

The President answers:

> **"Even if the law permits this punishment, should the Republic still enforce it?"**

Thus, the pardoning power is not intended to correct judicial errors but to provide a **constitutional safety valve** against excessive harshness and to uphold the humanitarian values embedded in the Constitution.

> **Core Constitutional Principle**
> 
> _Justice determines guilt; constitutional mercy determines whether the punishment should ultimately be enforced._

---

# Constitutional Basis

## Article 72

The President may grant:

- Pardon
- Reprieve
- Respite
- Remission
- Commutation

---

# Scope of Article 72

The President may exercise this power in:

### 1. Court-Martial Cases

Punishments awarded by Military Courts.

---

### 2. Union Executive Matters

Offences relating to laws where the executive power of the Union extends.

---

### 3. Death Sentences

The President may grant clemency in **all death sentence cases**, irrespective of whether the offence relates to Union or State law.

---

# Forms of Clemency

|Type|Meaning|
|---|---|
|**Pardon**|Completely removes conviction, sentence and disqualifications.|
|**Commutation**|Substitutes a lesser form of punishment (e.g., death → life imprisonment).|
|**Remission**|Reduces the duration of the sentence without changing its nature.|
|**Respite**|Awards a lesser sentence due to special circumstances (pregnancy, disability, age, illness, etc.).|
|**Reprieve**|Temporarily postpones execution of a sentence, especially a death sentence.|

---

# Why does the Constitution provide Pardoning Power?

The Constitution recognizes that:

- No judicial system is infallible.
- Justice should not become mechanical.
- Punishment should remain humane.
- Executive governance may require consideration of factors beyond the judicial record.
- The Republic must retain the capacity for mercy.

---

# Constitutional Significance

The Pardoning Power promotes:

- Human dignity (Article 21)
- Reformative justice
- Constitutional morality
- Compassion
- Public interest
- Executive responsibility

---

# Nature of the Power

The President:

- Does **not** sit in appeal over the Judiciary.
- Does **not** ordinarily re-appreciate evidence.
- Exercises constitutional clemency based on broader humanitarian and public interest considerations.

The power is exercised on the **aid and advice of the Council of Ministers**.

---

# Factors Generally Considered

The Executive may consider:

- Undue delay in execution.
- Mental illness.
- Terminal illness.
- Age.
- Pregnancy.
- Humanitarian grounds.
- Possibility of reformation.
- Diplomatic or national interest considerations.
- Constitutional morality.

---

# Judicial Review

The Pardoning Power is **not absolute**.

Courts may review the decision if it is:

- Mala fide.
- Arbitrary.
- Based on irrelevant considerations.
- Discriminatory.
- Passed without application of mind.

Courts **do not** ordinarily substitute their own decision for that of the Executive.

---

# Landmark Judgments

## Maru Ram v. Union of India (1980)

**Held**

The President exercises pardoning power on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers.

---

## Kehar Singh v. Union of India (1989)

**Held**

The President may examine the merits of the case while deciding a mercy petition.

The power is constitutional and humanitarian rather than appellate.

---

## Epuru Sudhakar v. Government of Andhra Pradesh (2006)

**Held**

Exercise of clemency is subject to judicial review.

Grounds include:

- Mala fide
- Arbitrariness
- Irrelevant considerations
- Discrimination

---

## Shatrughan Chauhan v. Union of India (2014)

**Held**

Excessive delay in deciding mercy petitions may justify commutation of a death sentence.

**Constitutional Principle**

Article 21 protects even condemned prisoners.

# President vs Governor

|Aspect|President (Article 72)|Governor (Article 161)|
|---|---|---|
|Court-Martial|✅ Yes|❌ No|
|Death Sentence|✅ Can pardon, reprieve, respite, remission or commute|✅ Can suspend, remit or commute, but cannot grant a full pardon for a death sentence|
|Union Laws|✅ Yes|❌ No|
|State Laws|Death sentence cases only|✅ Yes|

# LEA Reflection _(Not for Answer Writing)_

The Pardoning Power demonstrates that the Constitution distinguishes **legal justice** from **constitutional mercy**.

The Judiciary determines legal guilt and punishment according to law. The President, acting through the elected Executive, determines whether broader humanitarian, constitutional or public interest considerations justify clemency.

Thus, the power preserves both the **authority of the Judiciary** and the **compassion of the Republic**, making it one of the finest examples of constitutional checks and balances.
