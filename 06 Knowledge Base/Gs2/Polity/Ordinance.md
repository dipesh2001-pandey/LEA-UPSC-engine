# Purpose

The Ordinance-making power enables the Executive to respond immediately when urgent legislative action is required but Parliament is not in session.

It is an **extraordinary constitutional power**, intended to ensure continuity of governance rather than to replace the ordinary legislative process.

---

# Constitutional Philosophy

The Constitution balances two competing principles:

- **Parliamentary Supremacy in law-making**
- **Continuity of Governance during emergencies**

Ordinarily, Parliament alone makes laws. However, when immediate action is essential and Parliament is unavailable, the Constitution temporarily authorizes the Executive to legislate.

> **Core Constitutional Principle**
> 
> _The Ordinance-making power is an emergency legislative mechanism designed to preserve constitutional governance, not executive supremacy._

---

# Constitutional Basis

## Article 123

The President may promulgate an Ordinance when:

- Both Houses of Parliament are not in session, **or**
- One House is not in session (making legislation impossible),

**and**

The President is satisfied that circumstances exist requiring **immediate action**.

> **Note:** Presidential satisfaction is exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers (Article 74).

---

# Nature of Ordinance

An Ordinance:

- Has the same force and effect as an Act of Parliament.
- Is temporary.
- Must be laid before Parliament.
- Is subject to judicial review.
- Cannot amend the Constitution under Article 368.

---

# Conditions for Promulgation

Both conditions must exist simultaneously:

## 1. Parliament is not in a position to legislate.

AND

## 2. Immediate action is necessary.

Absence of either condition makes the exercise constitutionally vulnerable.

---

# Duration

An Ordinance ceases to operate:

- **Six weeks after Parliament reassembles**, unless approved earlier.

It may also cease earlier if:

- Disapproved by either House.
- Withdrawn by the President.

---

# Approval Process

```text
Parliament Not in Session
            │
            ▼
Immediate Necessity
            │
            ▼
President Promulgates Ordinance
            │
            ▼
Parliament Reassembles
            │
            ▼
Must be laid before Parliament
            │
            ▼
Approved → Becomes Act

Rejected / Not Passed → Lapses
```

---

# Limitations

The Ordinance power:

- Cannot be used when Parliament is in session.
- Cannot permanently replace legislation.
- Cannot bypass parliamentary debate as a matter of routine.
- Is subject to constitutional limitations.
- Is subject to judicial review.

---

# Judicial Interpretation

## D.C. Wadhwa v. State of Bihar (1987)

### Facts

The State repeatedly re-promulgated ordinances without placing them before the legislature.

### Held

Repeated re-promulgation is a **fraud on the Constitution**.

### Principle

The Ordinance power is intended only for exceptional circumstances.

---

## Krishna Kumar Singh v. State of Bihar (2017)

### Held

- Ordinance-making power is subject to judicial review.
- Satisfaction is **not absolute**.
- Immediate necessity must genuinely exist.
- Re-promulgation is unconstitutional except in exceptional circumstances.
- The Executive must justify the necessity behind the Ordinance when replacing it with legislation.

### Relevance vs Adequacy Doctrine

The Court generally examines:

- **Relevance** of the material leading to the satisfaction.

The Court ordinarily **does not assess the adequacy** of that material.

Thus, judicial review is limited but real.

---

# Constitutional Safeguards Against Misuse

- Temporary nature.
- Mandatory parliamentary approval.
- Six-week limitation.
- Judicial review.
- Constitutional conventions.
- Democratic accountability of the Council of Ministers.

---

# Constitutional Concerns

Frequent promulgation may lead to:

- Executive overreach.
- Weakening of parliamentary deliberation.
- Circumvention of democratic scrutiny.
- Legislative bypass.

Hence, the power must remain an exception rather than the norm.
